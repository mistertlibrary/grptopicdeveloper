Landmarks come in many forms. Consider these categories:

Court Cases Supreme Court decisions, international tribunals, precedent-setting verdicts
Protests & Movements Mass mobilizations, viral campaigns, sustained activism
Policy Changes Laws passed, regulations enacted, treaties signed
Attacks & Crises Acts of violence, natural disasters, public health emergencies
Discoveries Scientific breakthroughs, technological releases, revelations
Cultural Shifts Viral moments, boycotts, cancellations, platform changes

How to recognize a landmark:

  • Can you point to a "before" and "after"?
  • Did it spark ongoing debate or inspire similar events elsewhere?
  • Did it force institutions (governments, corporations, schools) to respond?
  • Is it still referenced today when discussing your umbrella topic?

Be specific. Not "Ukraine war" but "Russia invades Ukraine." Not "police shooting" but "Death of George Floyd."

How do I know if my event is too broad?

Warning signs your event is too broad:

  • It spans multiple years (e.g., "Syrian Civil War" or "COVID-19 Pandemic")
  • It contains hundreds of distinct incidents (e.g., "Black Lives Matter Movement")
  • It's a trend rather than a moment (e.g., "Rise of Social Media")

How to narrow it:

  • From "COVID-19 Pandemic" ‚Üí "WHO declares COVID-19 a pandemic (March 2020)"
  • From "Syrian Civil War" ‚Üí "Chemical weapons attack in Ghouta (August 2013)"
  • From "Black Lives Matter" ‚Üí "Death of George Floyd (May 2020)"

A good landmark has a specific date and a single identifiable moment even if its effects ripple outward.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Students often struggle with these issues:

  • Too recent: Events from the last 6 months lack scholarly analysis. Choose events at least 1 year old.
  • Too personal/local: Your town's new ordinance likely lacks national significance unless it sparked broader debate.
  • Generic wording: "The protests" or "the controversy" won't help you or your instructor identify your event. Use proper names.
  • Confusing cause with effect: "#MeToo movement" is the effect; "Harvey Weinstein allegations go public" is the event that sparked it.
  • Not actually post-2006: Double-check your dates. The assignment requires 2007 or later.

How do I know if my paragraph is strong enough?

Check for these elements:

  • Specificity: Does every sentence contain proper nouns, dates, or concrete details?
  • Causation: Do you explain why things happened, not just what happened?
  • Context: Can a reader unfamiliar with this event understand its significance?
  • Argument: Are you making a claim about significance or just summarizing?

Things to avoid: Vague language ("many people," "society," "things changed"), missing dates, no source-based evidence.
